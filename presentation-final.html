Root Causes & Risk Factors

06 / 21
Historical Context

Centuries of Cultural Disruption

The diabetes epidemic in Native American communities did not happen by chance—it is the result of centuries of forced cultural disruption. Historical trauma from colonization, forced relocation to reservations, and the boarding school era created intergenerational mistrust of healthcare systems (Walls & Whitbeck, 2012; Gone & Trimble, 2012).

Dietary Transition

From Traditional Foods to Commodity Rations

Perhaps most significantly, there was a forced dietary transition from traditional, healthy foods to government commodity foods high in sugar, refined flour, and lard (Wedekind et al., 2021). This shift from lean proteins, vegetables and whole grains to processed foods like frybread directly fueled the diabetes epidemic.

Cultural Attribution

"White Man's Sickness"

Some Native patients describe diabetes as a white man's sickness—a disease brought by cultural disruption (Jernigan et al., 2020). Understanding these root causes is essential for providing effective, compassionate care.

Figure 2: Causal pathway to diabetes epidemic. Source: Wedekind et al. (2021)

Major Complications
36% have cardiovascular disease (IHS, 2024)
31% with chronic kidney disease; ESRD incidence 1.6× higher than non-Hispanic whites (IHS, 2024; Wedekind et al., 2021)
24% with diabetic retinopathy among those examined (IHS, 2024)
4% have documented lower extremity amputation (IHS, 2024)

Cultural stoicism and fatalistic attitudes toward diabetes may lead to late presentation and delayed treatment, exacerbating complications (Wedekind et al., 2021).

Traditional Healing & Complementary Medicine

08 / 21
Integration

Bridging Traditional & Western Medicine

Traditional healing practices are deeply woven into Native American approaches to diabetes management (Jernigan et al., 2020). Many patients use both traditional medicine and Western treatments in complementary ways—and when done respectfully, this integration can improve outcomes.

Practices

Healing Ceremonies & Herbal Remedies

Medicine Men & Tribal Healers: Use rituals, prayer, and herbal remedies passed through generations (Jernigan et al., 2020)
Smudging: Burning sage, cedar, sweetgrass provides spiritual cleansing believed to restore balance
Sweat Lodges: Detoxification ceremonies (assess cardiovascular/diabetes status first)
Herbal Remedies: Sage tea, bitter melon, traditional plants for blood sugar management

Progressive hospitals accommodate ceremonial practices with proper permissions, recognizing their importance to healing and patient dignity (Jernigan et al., 2020).

Nursing Considerations

Respectful Collaboration

Respectfully inquire about complementary medicine use
Check for potential drug interactions with diabetes medications
Collaborate with traditional healers when appropriate
Accommodate ceremonial practices with proper permissions

Health Beliefs & Medicine Wheel Approach

10 / 21
Holistic Worldview

Four Dimensions of Health

The Medicine Wheel represents the holistic worldview central to Native American health beliefs. It has four quadrants—Physical, Mental, Emotional, and Spiritual—all must be in balance for true health. Diabetes is a manifestation of imbalance across all four dimensions (Jernigan et al., 2020).

Four Dimensions of Health
Physical
Blood sugar management, medication adherence, exercise
Mental
Diabetes education, understanding disease process
Emotional
Family support, coping with chronic illness
Spiritual
Traditional ceremonies, connection to culture
Nursing Note: Nurses must explore these beliefs respectfully and align diabetes care plans with patients' cultural worldview, using concepts of balance and harmony when discussing treatment goals.

Family Dynamics & Decision-Making

12 / 21
Collectivist Culture

The Central Role of Family

For Native American patients with diabetes, family is central to all healthcare decisions. Family extends beyond the nuclear unit to include extended relatives, elders, and community members. Decision-making is a collective, consultative process—not individual (Jernigan et al., 2020).

Elder Authority

Respecting Traditional Hierarchy

Elders' opinions carry tremendous weight and may approve major treatment decisions (Jernigan et al., 2020)

Multiple relatives may attend appointments—welcome this, not view as crowding

In some tribes, matriarchal figures lead health decisions and must be included in care

Collectivist culture means family/tribe needs take precedence over individual preferences—this is a strength to leverage (Jernigan et al., 2020)

Figure 5 — Multigenerational family structure

Nursing Strategies

Family-Centered Diabetes Care

View the family as essential allies in diabetes care, not obstacles

Frame benefits in family terms: "This insulin helps you stay strong to care for your grandchildren"

Involve entire family in diabetes education sessions

Facilitate family consultations when needed for major treatment decisions

Remember: Extended family structure includes relatives, elders, and community

Gender Issues & Middle-Aged Males

14 / 21
Developmental Stage

Generativity vs. Stagnation (Ages 40-65)

Middle-aged Native American men face unique cultural and gender-based challenges with diabetes. This age group corresponds to Erikson's (1982) Generativity versus Stagnation stage, where men focus on contributing to family and community. Diabetes threatens this generative role, requiring sensitive reframing.

Erikson, E. H. (1982). The life cycle completed: A review. W. W. Norton & Company.

Cultural Stoicism

Barriers to Care-Seeking

Cultural conditioning promotes stoicism—expressing pain/illness seen as weakness (Wedekind et al., 2021)

Men under-report diabetes symptoms like neuropathy pain or vision changes

Healthcare-seeking viewed as weakness, causing reluctance to attend appointments

Provider/protector role identity threatened by chronic illness (Erikson, 1982)

Reframing Strategy: Reframe diabetes self-care as fulfilling the provider role: "Taking insulin helps you stay strong for your family. Blood sugar monitoring shows responsibility as a father."

Nursing Approach

Strengths-Based Messaging

Frame diabetes management as strength, not weakness

Emphasize family responsibility and community contribution

Enable continued generative work of guiding next generation

Acknowledge historical trauma's impact on healthcare trust

Spirituality & Religion in Healing

16 / 21
Inseparable from Healing

Spirituality as Central to Care

Spirituality is inseparable from diabetes healing in Native American culture. Many practice both traditional Native spirituality and Christianity—syncretism is common, and both traditions provide spiritual support for diabetes management (Jernigan et al., 2020).

Spiritual Practices

Ceremonies & Sacred Items

Bedside rituals, prayer circles, healing songs, drumming for diabetic patients

Sun Dance and Night Chant offer spiritual strength for chronic illness

Medicine bags, eagle feathers, sacred herbs never removed without permission

Many view diabetes as spiritual test sent by Creator (Jernigan et al., 2020)

Integration Note: Spiritual ceremonies and connection to land/ancestors provide mental health support essential for diabetes management (Jernigan et al., 2020). Spirituality is central to holistic healing.
Historical Trauma

Boarding Schools & Mistrust

Historical trauma from boarding schools and forced assimilation created profound mistrust of healthcare systems (Gone & Trimble, 2012; Walls & Whitbeck, 2012). Nurses must acknowledge this trauma explicitly and address through transparent, respectful care.

Figure 7 — Spiritual practices in healing

Nursing Protocols

Respect & Accommodation

Respect sacred items at all times—never remove without permission

Accommodate spiritual practices when possible with hospital permissions

Recognize that spiritual healing aids physical diabetes outcomes

Focus on positive healing rather than direct death talk (culturally taboo)

Diet, Nutrition & Food Access

18 / 21
Traditional vs. Modern Diet

From Ancestral Foods to Commodity Rations

Traditional Native diets were diabetes-protective: lean proteins from wild game/fish, the Three Sisters (corn, beans, squash), berries, nuts, seasonal vegetables. These unprocessed foods provided balanced nutrition and natural blood sugar regulation (Wedekind et al., 2021).

Commodity Foods Impact

Frybread & the Diabetes Epidemic

Forced relocation created dependency on government commodity foods high in refined flour, sugar, and lard. Frybread, now considered traditional, originated from these rations and fuels the diabetes epidemic (Wedekind et al., 2021).

Nursing Strategies

Culturally Responsive Nutrition Care

Frame dietary changes as returning to ancestral foods (SDPI, 2022)

Involve entire family in meal planning for diabetes

Suggest healthier preparation methods (baking vs frying)

Understanding Differences

Shows respect, not disengagement (Jernigan et al., 2020)

Indicates thoughtful processing, not lack of understanding (Jernigan et al., 2020)

Preferred method—use diabetes success stories from community

Building Trust

Overcoming Historical Trauma

Boarding schools and unethical medical experiments created profound mistrust of healthcare systems (Gone & Trimble, 2012; Walls & Whitbeck, 2012). Nurses must acknowledge this trauma explicitly and build trust through transparency.

Cultural Concept: Indian Time reflects flexible, present-focused time orientation (Jernigan et al., 2020). Be adaptable with scheduling rather than labeling patients as noncompliant.
