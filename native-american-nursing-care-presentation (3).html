Historical & Social Causes

  • Historical Trauma: Colonization, forced relocation, boarding schools
  • Healthcare Mistrust: Intergenerational trauma from past abuses
  • Extreme Poverty: Limited economic opportunities on reservations
  • Access Barriers: Geographic isolation, inadequate IHS funding

Dietary & Lifestyle Factors

  • Commodity Foods: Government-supplied processed foods (sugar, lard, white flour)
  • Traditional Diet Lost: Shift from lean game, vegetables, whole grains
  • Food Deserts: Limited access to fresh, healthy foods
  • "White Man's Sickness": Cultural attribution to imposed lifestyle changes

Severe Diabetes Complications

  • Cardiovascular Disease: Heart attacks, strokes - leading cause of death
  • Kidney Failure: Diabetic nephropathy requiring dialysis
  • Lower Extremity Amputations: Diabetic neuropathy and poor wound healing
  • Vision Loss: Diabetic retinopathy causing blindness
  • Neuropathy: Painful nerve damage in extremities

Medicine Men & Healers

Tribal healers use rituals, prayer, and herbal remedies alongside Western diabetes medicine. Show respect and collaborate when appropriate for integrated diabetes care.

Smudging & Purification

Burning sacred herbs (sage, cedar, sweetgrass) for spiritual cleansing. Many hospitals now accommodate these practices for diabetic patients during care.

Sweat Lodges

Steam bath ceremonies for detoxification and spiritual healing. Assess cardiovascular status and diabetes control before patient participation.

Herbal Medicine for Diabetes

Traditional plants for blood sugar management. Always ask about herbal supplements (bitter melon, fenugreek, etc.) to check for interactions with diabetes medications.

Image 13: Multi-generational Native American family - Extended family involvement in diabetes care decisions. Elders, spouses, children, and extended relatives all participate in healthcare planning. Source: Office of Minority Health - Family & Community Health Programs & CDC Native American Health Initiatives Image Library

Extended Family Involvement in Diabetes

  • Diabetes management decisions are family affairs
  • Multiple relatives provide input on treatment plans
  • Welcome and involve support system in diabetes education
  • Family helps with dietary changes and medication adherence

Elder Hierarchy & Matriarchs

  • Elders' opinions carry great weight in diabetes care decisions
  • May need elder approval for insulin or major changes
  • Matriarchal leaders in some tribes influence health choices
  • Facilitate family consultations for diabetes care plans

"In a collectivist culture, diabetes management decisions are made in the context of 'What is best for the family or tribe,' not just the individual. This is strength, not a barrier."

üí°
Nursing Tip for Diabetes: Frame insulin and treatment benefits in terms of family support: "This diabetes medication will help you stay strong to continue caring for your grandchildren and supporting your family."

Gender & Middle-Aged Males with Diabetes

Presenter: Mathew Moslow

üë®üèΩ‚Äçü¶≥üí™üèΩ
Ages 40-65
Gener ativity vs. Stagnation

Image 14: Middle-aged Native American man - Erikson's Generativity stage. Focus on contributing to family/community while managing diabetes. Provider role identity central to self-concept. Source: CDC Image Library - Native American Men's Health & National Institute on Minority Health and Health Disparities (NIMHD)

Stoicism & Diabetes Symptoms

Men may under-report diabetes complications (neuropathy pain, vision changes) as expressing illness is seen as weakness. Observe non-verbal cues carefully and explain how managing symptoms prevents serious complications.

Provider/Protector Role Threatened

Diabetes and its complications threaten the provider role. Men may feel they're failing if unable to work. Frame diabetes management as strength: "Controlling your diabetes shows responsibility to your family."

Reluctance to Seek Care

Healthcare-seeking viewed as weakness. Men may delay diabetes appointments, skip glucose monitoring, or avoid discussing complications. Emphasize that managing diabetes enables family care.

Generativity vs. Stagnation

Ages 40-65: Contributing to family and community is central. Diabetes can create feelings of stagnation. Help patients see diabetes management as generative act - staying healthy to guide next generation.

Traditional vs. Modern Diabetes-Impact

  • Traditional: Wild game, fish, Three Sisters (corn, beans, squash), berries, nuts - diabetes-protective
  • Modern: Frybread, commodity foods, refined sugar, lard - drives diabetes epidemic
  • Impact: Dietary shift directly correlates with diabetes surge

Diabetes-Friendly Nutrition Strategies

  • Frame as "return to ancestral foods" for diabetes control
  • My Native Plate & SDPI programs - culturally tailored
  • Acknowledge food deserts, poverty, lactose intolerance
  • Involve family in meal planning for diabetes
  • Suggest healthier frybread preparation (baked not fried)

Communication & Special Considerations in Diabetes Care

Presenter: Shanique Wilson

üë©üèª‚Äç‚öïÔ∏è ü§ùüèΩ üë®üèΩ

Image 18: Culturally sensitive nurse-patient communication - Building trust through respect for communication styles: Limited eye contact, allowing silence, using storytelling for diabetes education. Source: IHS Patient-Centered Communication Training Resources & Office of Minority Health Cultural Competency Curriculum

Building Trust for Diabetes Care

  • Acknowledge historical trauma and healthcare mistrust
  • Be transparent about diabetes treatment plans
  • Keep promises (follow-up calls, appointment reminders)
  • Own mistakes immediately - builds credibility

Diabetes Communication Nuances

  • Limited eye contact = respect, not disengagement
  • Silence = thoughtful processing about diabetes plan
  • Use storytelling - share diabetes success stories
  • "Yes" may mean "I hear you," not agreement to insulin
  • Humor as important diabetes coping mechanism

Diabetes Access Barriers

  • Geographic isolation - long distances to IHS clinics
  • Limited transportation for diabetes appointments
  • Financial constraints - insulin, testing supplies costs
  • IHS funding limitations for diabetes care
  • "Indian Time" - flexible time orientation, be adaptable

Diabetes Care Solutions

  • Telehealth for glucose monitoring, diabetes education
  • Coordinate with local IHS clinics for continuity
  • Involve Community Health Representatives as liaisons
  • Connect with SDPI diabetes programs
  • Talking circles for peer diabetes support
